Understanding NB MRR Pacing

A step-by-step guide to how baseline performance, incremental lift, and annualized targets work together. When you’re ready to model your own funnel, open the NB MRR pacing calculator.

1 Baseline: The Starting Point

Your funnel produces a flat number of new bookings every month. This is the baseline — what happens with no new improvements, just running the current machine.

Important: Throughout this page, all dollar amounts represent incremental NB MRR — the new recurring revenue from newly acquired users each month. This is not total recognized revenue, which would include the compounding base of all prior months’ recurring revenue minus churn. This model only tracks new bookings added each month.
Example: Your funnel produces 1,000 new bookings/month at $50 AOV.
That means baseline monthly NB MRR = 1,000 × $50 = $50,000/month of new recurring revenue.

Since the baseline is the same every month, the annual math is simple:

Baseline monthly NB MRR   =  $50,000
Baseline annual NB MRR    =  $50,000 × 12 = $600,000

Key point: Every month is identical, so monthly × 12 = annual. No surprises.

2 Incremental Lift: Accumulating Month Over Month

Now imagine your team ships improvements — optimizations, new features, conversion lifts. Each month, these improvements deliver an average amount of incremental lift that permanently increases the monthly output going forward.

Example: Improvements average $2,500/month in additional NB MRR on top of baseline.
By August you’ve accumulated 1 month of lift. By September, 2 months of lift are stacked. By July (month 12), all 12 months of lift are in place.

Notice how each month is different. August produces $52,500 but July produces $80,000. The output ramps up as incremental lift accumulates.

3 Three Numbers That Sound Similar but Aren't

This is where the confusion happens. There are three different “monthly” numbers people reference:

A) Exit Monthly Rate (Month 12)

The rate in July, when all 12 months of incremental lift have accumulated. This is the best month — the run-rate you exit the year at, and it becomes the baseline monthly rate for the following fiscal year.

Exit monthly = $50,000 + 12 × $2,500 = $80,000

B) Average Monthly Rate

The annual total divided by 12. Since early months have less accumulated lift, this is lower than the exit rate.

Average monthly = Annual total ÷ 12 = $795,000 ÷ 12 = $66,250

C) Baseline Monthly Rate

The flat rate with zero incremental lift. This is your floor.

Baseline monthly = $50,000
Key point: The exit rate ($80K) is NOT the same as the average ($66.25K). If someone says “monthly” without specifying which one, ask: “Do you mean the exit rate or the average?”

5 The Incremental Ramp Formula

There's a clean formula for the total incremental over a year with a linear ramp. If the average monthly lift is W:

Total incremental = W × (1 + 2 + 3 + ... + 12) = W × 78

In our example: $2,500 × 78 = $195,000 total incremental across the year.

Compare that to what you'd get if the full lift was in place from day one:

If full lift from day 1: 12 × W × 12 = $2,500 × 144 = $360,000

The ramp captures 54% of the full-year incremental NB MRR (78/144) because lift takes time to accumulate. This ratio (78/144 = 6.5/12) is always the same for a linear 12-month ramp.

Rule of thumb: With incremental lift ramping evenly, you realize about 54% of the in-year incremental NB MRR you'd get if all improvement was in place from day one. The other 46% is “deferred” to the ramp period. The calculator’s In-Year Annual scenario mode applies this same 78/144 ramp logic to your low/high targets — try it with your numbers.

6 Putting It All Together: “10% Lift” on What?

When someone says “target a 10% lift,” the first question is: 10% of what? There are three common interpretations, and they produce wildly different outcomes. You must agree on which one you mean before the math makes sense.

Three ways to interpret a “10% lift”:

• A) Exit Growth: The month-12 run-rate is 10% above baseline. The lift ramps linearly and the in-year total falls out of that ramp.

• B) In-Year Annual: The annualized NB MRR total is 10% above baseline. This requires a larger monthly lift to compensate for the ramp.

• C) Compounding Monthly: Each month’s output is 10% higher than the previous month’s. The lift accelerates because the base it’s applied to keeps growing.

The same “10%” target produces annual incremental NB MRR ranging from $65K to $1.15M depending on interpretation.

Which interpretation is right?

A) and B) are the two realistic planning models. They both use a linear ramp — each month, a fixed dollar increment of improvement lands and persists. The difference is just which outcome the 10% target anchors to (exit rate vs. annual total). The calculator supports both via its Exit Growth and In-Year Annual modes.

C) is usually unrealistic for funnel optimization. Compounding assumes each month’s 10% lift applies to the already-lifted run-rate, which implies your improvements multiply rather than add. In practice, funnel optimizations (conversion lifts, traffic gains, feature launches) add a fixed increment to the baseline — they don’t make the next month’s improvement 10% larger too. If someone says “10% monthly growth,” make sure they don’t mean compounding — the numbers diverge fast.
Always clarify: When someone says “target a 10% lift,” ask: “10% exit growth, 10% in-year annual growth, or 10% compounding monthly?” The answer changes the result by an order of magnitude.

Regardless of interpretation, the same core truths hold:

  • Avg monthly × 12 = Annualized — always checks out
  • Exit rate × 12 ≠ Annualized — exit rate is higher than average
  • The exit rate tells you where you end the year (your new run-rate going into next year)
  • The annual total tells you what you actually produced this year
Both numbers matter:
• Annualized = what you deliver this year (in-year NB MRR impact)
• Exit Rate = your run-rate entering next year (carrying value going forward)

7 The 54% Rule: Why Ramps Realize Less Than You Think

One of the most counterintuitive parts of the ramp model is how much incremental NB MRR you don't capture in-year. Even though you achieve the full lift by year-end, you only realize about 54% of what you'd get if all improvement had been in place from day one.

The two scenarios side by side

Imagine the average monthly lift is $2,500/month in NB MRR. Compare what happens if the full lift is in place from August 1 vs. accumulating gradually:

Both scenarios end July at the same $30,000/month NB MRR exit rate. But because the ramp builds gradually, the in-year incremental NB MRR total is much lower.

Where does the 54% come from?

Day-one incremental  = W × 12 months × 12 lifts = W × 144
Ramped incremental   = W × (1 + 2 + 3 + … + 12) = W × 78

Realized ratio      = 78 ÷ 144 = 54.2%

The number 78 comes from the triangular number formula: n(n+1)/2 = 12 × 13 / 2 = 78. The number 144 is simply 12 × 12. This ratio is always the same for a linear 12-month ramp, regardless of the lift amount.

Value% of Day-One

NB MRR “deferred” to future years$165,00046%

Why this matters for planning

Don’t confuse the exit rate with the annual impact.

• If your goal is $360K in incremental in-year NB MRR, $2,500/month of average lift won’t get you there — it gets you $195K in-year. You'd need to roughly double the lift or deliver it faster.

• However, the NB MRR exit rate is still $30K/month. So next fiscal year, you carry that full $30K × 12 = $360K into your NB MRR baseline. The value isn’t lost — it’s deferred.

Formula: n(n+1)/2 ÷ n² = (n+1) / (2n). As the ramp gets longer, the realized % slowly approaches 50%.

8 Earlier Lift Is Worth More Than Later Lift

Not all lift is created equal. Improvement that lands in August contributes for 12 remaining months. Improvement that lands in July only contributes for 1 month. Same lift, dramatically different in-year impact.

Lift landing in August is worth 12× more in-year NB MRR than the same lift landing in July. The first month’s improvement delivers $30,000 in annualized incremental NB MRR; the last month’s delivers just $2,500.

In-year value of lift = W × (13 − month number)
August lift: $2,500 × 12 = $30,000
July lift:   $2,500 × 1  = $2,500

What this means for prioritization

Front-load your biggest improvements.

• A large improvement delivered early (e.g., a 5% conversion lift in August) produces far more in-year NB MRR than the same improvement delivered in March.

• Delaying improvement by 3 months costs 25% of its in-year NB MRR. $2,500/month of lift delayed from Aug to Nov loses $7,500 in in-year NB MRR ($30K → $22.5K).

• Small, fast improvements beat large, slow ones for in-year NB MRR impact. Two $1,250/month lifts delivered in Aug and Sep together produce $28,750 in in-year NB MRR ($15,000 + $13,750) — more than a single $2,500/month lift delayed until Nov ($22,500).

However, remember that all lift — early or late — carries the same NB MRR exit rate into the following year. A July improvement still adds $2,500/month to next year's baseline. The timing only affects this year's annualized NB MRR total.

Bottom line: The NB MRR exit rate doesn’t care when improvement lands. The annualized NB MRR total does. If you’re optimizing for this year’s in-year NB MRR and realized revenue, prioritize speed. For establishing next year's baseline, the timing matters less.

9 From NB MRR to In-Year Recognized Revenue

Everything above tracks NB MRR — the new recurring revenue added each month from newly acquired users. But there’s a critical next question: how much actual revenue do those new bookings generate within the fiscal year? The calculator’s weekly cohort table applies this logic to your inputs (each cohort’s NB MRR × remaining weeks in the fiscal year).

The answer depends on when each cohort of new bookings lands, because each month’s new bookings keep paying for every subsequent month in the year.

How a single month’s NB MRR becomes recognized revenue

If you add $50,000 in NB MRR in August (month 1 of the fiscal year), those subscribers pay every month for the rest of the year:

August NB MRR recognized in-year = $50,000 × 12 remaining months = $600,000

But $50,000 in NB MRR added in July (month 12) only contributes for 1 month:

July NB MRR recognized in-year = $50,000 × 1 remaining month = $50,000

Same NB MRR rate, 12× difference in recognized revenue — entirely due to timing.

Baseline: flat NB MRR → recognized revenue

With a flat $50,000/month NB MRR baseline, each month’s cohort contributes recognized revenue for the remaining months of the year:

In-year recognized revenue = NB MRR × (12 + 11 + 10 + … + 1) = $50,000 × 78 = $3,900,000
Key distinction:
• Annualized NB MRR = $50K × 12 = $600,000 — the total new recurring revenue added across the year.
• In-year recognized revenue from those new bookings = $50K × 78 = $3,900,000 — the actual revenue those cohorts generate within the fiscal year.

The recognized revenue is 6.5× the annualized NB MRR because earlier cohorts keep paying for many months.

The conversion factor: 78 / 12 = 6.5×

For a flat monthly NB MRR rate, the relationship is always:

In-year recognized revenue = Annualized NB MRR × 6.5

Why 6.5?  The average month’s cohort contributes for (12 + 1) / 2 = 6.5 months.

This 6.5× multiplier applies to both the baseline and any incremental NB MRR. So if you know the annualized incremental NB MRR, you can estimate the in-year recognized revenue from those new bookings.

Weekly NB MRR → recognized revenue

If you track NB MRR on a weekly basis, first convert to monthly (the calculator’s funnel inputs use weekly traffic, then × 52/12 for monthly NB MRR):

Monthly NB MRR = Weekly NB MRR × 52 / 12 ≈ Weekly NB MRR × 4.33

Then apply the same recognized revenue logic. For example, if weekly NB MRR is $11,538:

Monthly NB MRR ≈ $11,538 × 4.33 ≈ $50,000
Annualized NB MRR = $50,000 × 12 = $600,000
In-year recognized revenue = $600,000 × 6.5 = $3,900,000

G Glossary

NB MRR (New Booking Monthly Recurring Revenue)
The new recurring revenue generated from newly acquired users in a given month. This is incremental revenue only — it does not include the compounding base of prior months’ recurring revenue or account for churn/cancellations. The annualized total is the sum of 12 monthly increments of new booking MRR.
Baseline
The expected monthly output (activations, bookings, or NB MRR) assuming no new improvements ship. A flat, repeating monthly rate derived from the current confidence-adjusted funnel.
Confidence-Adjusted Rate
A funnel conversion rate multiplied by a confidence percentage to account for uncertainty. For example, a 2.5% Traffic-to-Activation rate at 85% confidence = 2.125% adjusted rate.
Incremental
The additional output above baseline produced by shipping improvements (optimizations, conversion lifts, new features). Each month’s average lift adds a permanent increase that persists for the rest of the year.
Incremental Lift
The average amount of improvement delivered each month. This is the aggregate effect of all optimizations, A/B test results, and feature launches — not a single discrete win, but the average monthly contribution from the team’s improvement efforts.
Ramp
The pattern created by incremental lift accumulating month over month. Month 1 has 1 month of lift, month 2 has 2 months stacked, etc. The total output increases each month as lift accumulates.
Exit Rate (Month 12 Rate)
The monthly output in the final month of the fiscal year, when all 12 months of incremental lift have accumulated. This is the highest monthly value and represents the run-rate you carry into the next fiscal year.
Average Monthly Rate
The FY annual total divided by 12. Because of the ramp, this is lower than the exit rate but higher than the baseline. This is the number where average × 12 = annual total always holds.
Annualized Total
The sum of all 12 monthly values across the fiscal year (Aug–Jul). Captures the actual incremental NB MRR produced during the year, including the ramp effect. Not the same as exit rate × 12.
Annual Incremental
The difference between a scenario’s annualized total and the baseline annualized total. Represents the total additional NB MRR gained from incremental lift across the fiscal year, accounting for the ramp. Because lift accumulates gradually, this is ~54% of what you’d get if all improvement was in place from day one.
FY26 Exit / FY27 Entrance
The annualized NB MRR run-rate at the end of FY26, which becomes the starting baseline for FY27 planning. Split across workstreams.
Low / High Scenario
Two improvement assumptions representing a range of outcomes. Low (e.g., 5%) assumes conservative lift; High (e.g., 15%) assumes aggressive lift. Both apply the same ramp model, just with different lift amounts.
AOV (Average Order Value)
The average monthly recurring revenue per new booking. Used to convert bookings into NB MRR: NB MRR = Bookings × AOV.
Funnel
The conversion path from Traffic → Activations → Bookings → NB MRR. Each step has a conversion rate (potentially confidence-adjusted) that determines how many proceed to the next stage.
In-Year Recognized Revenue
The actual revenue generated within the fiscal year by new bookings. Each month’s NB MRR cohort contributes for the remaining months of the year, so earlier cohorts produce more recognized revenue. For a flat monthly NB MRR, in-year recognized revenue = annualized NB MRR × 6.5 (because the average cohort contributes for 6.5 months). Does not account for churn.
54% Rule of Thumb
With a linear 12-month ramp, you realize about 54% (78/144) of the in-year incremental NB MRR you’d get if all improvement was in place from day one. The rest is deferred to the ramp-up period.
